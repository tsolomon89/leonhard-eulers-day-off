**Timothy Solomon**

## Abstract

This paper proposes a single axiom from which the structural content of Gödel's incompleteness theorems, Turing's halting problem, Chaitin's algorithmic incompressibility, and the transcendence of fundamental mathematical constants can be derived as theorems. The axiom is: *there exist finite symbols that contain infinite information.* We call such symbols *symbolically necessary* and denote them with the operator ⊙. We show that the axiom is not metaphysical speculation but a physically constrained claim, grounded in the Bekenstein bound, Landauer's principle, and the thermodynamics of computation. We derive seven theorems from the axiom alone, culminating in a structural account of the gap between the material (dimensioned, quantitative, finite) and the immaterial (dimensionless, qualitative, complete). The paper claims that τ, π, and Ω are instances of ⊙, that e is not, and that the distinction is not arbitrary but forced by the axiom's internal logic.

---

## 1. The Axiom

We begin with one statement.

**Axiom (Symbolic Necessity).** There exist finite symbols that contain infinite information.

By "finite symbol" we mean a mark that can be written, stored, and communicated using bounded physical resources. A glyph on paper. A string of characters in memory. Something that takes up finite space and can be transmitted in finite time.

By "contain" we do not mean "encode" or "compress" or "point toward." We mean that the symbol IS the information. Not a summary. Not an approximation. Not a reference to a database where the real information lives. The symbol itself, in its totality, is the complete and perfect specification of an infinite object.

By "infinite information" we mean non-compressible, non-terminating, non-periodic specification. Information that cannot be finitely enumerated by any algorithmic process.

We call any such symbol *symbolically necessary* and write ⊙S to mean "S is a finite symbol containing infinite information."

That is the entire axiomatic foundation of this paper. Everything that follows is theory.

---

## 2. What the Axiom Says

The axiom makes a claim about the structure of reality. It says that there exist objects which are simultaneously:

Finite as symbols. You can write τ. You can write π. You can write Ω. Each is a single glyph or a short string. Each occupies a bounded region of any medium. Each can be communicated from one mind to another in a single act of reference.

Infinite as information. The decimal expansion of τ is non-terminating and non-periodic. No finite string of digits reproduces it. No algorithm outputs it and halts. The complete specification of what τ IS cannot be captured by any finite enumeration. The same holds for π and for Ω.

The axiom says both of these things are true at once. Not one or the other. Both. A finite mark on paper contains, right now, already, the complete specification of an infinite object. Not eventually. Not in principle. Not given enough time. Now.

This is a strange claim. It deserves scrutiny.

---

## 3. What the Axiom Does Not Say

The axiom does not say that infinity is physically realised. It does not say that somewhere in the universe there exists a complete material copy of the decimal expansion of τ. There does not. There cannot. (We will prove this.)

The axiom does not say that the symbol "τ" is magic. It says that the relationship between the symbol and its referent is fundamentally different from the relationship between, say, the numeral "7" and the number seven. "7" contains exactly the information it displays. "τ" contains infinitely more than any display can show. The symbol is complete. Every physical representation of its content is not.

The axiom does not say anything about consciousness, God, the soul, or the meaning of life. It says something about the structure of mathematical reference and its relationship to physical computation. What one does with that observation is a separate matter. We will note connections. We will not insist on them.

---

## 4. Theorem 1: Immateriality

**Theorem 1.** Any symbol satisfying ⊙ is not a physical object.

*Proof.* The Bekenstein bound establishes that the maximum information content of a region of space is finite and proportional to its surface area in Planck units:

$$S \leq \frac{2\pi RE}{\hbar c}$$

where $R$ is the radius of the region, $E$ is its total energy, $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, and $c$ is the speed of light. Any physical system confined to a finite region with finite energy contains at most a finite number of bits of information.

A symbol satisfying ⊙ contains infinite information. Therefore no physical system can instantiate the full content of such a symbol. The symbol as written on paper or stored in silicon contains a finite physical trace. The referent it names transcends any physical instantiation.

Such a symbol therefore has two aspects. Its material aspect (the glyph, the ink, the pixels) is finite and physical. Its informational aspect (what it refers to, completely and perfectly) is infinite and non-physical. We call the material aspect *dimensioned information* and the informational aspect *dimensionless information*.

This is not dualism. It is a structural observation. The same entity (the symbol τ) participates in two domains simultaneously: the domain of physical marks (where it is finite and bounded) and the domain of mathematical reference (where it is infinite and complete). ∎

---

## 5. Theorem 2: The Measurement Bound

**Theorem 2.** No physical process can extract the complete information content of a ⊙-symbol.

*Proof.* Computation is physical. Every step of a computation requires energy (Landauer's principle: at minimum $kT \ln 2$ per bit operation), time (bounded by the speed of light and the Margolus-Levitin limit on computation speed), and storage (bounded by Bekenstein).

The universe has finite age ($\sim 13.8 \times 10^9$ years), finite energy within any causal horizon, and finite spatial extent within any observable region. Therefore any physical computation that has been or will be performed within any causally connected region of the universe produces at most a finite output.

The information content of a ⊙-symbol is infinite. Therefore no physical computation completes the extraction. Every physical process that attempts to read out the content of a ⊙-symbol produces a finite prefix, a truncation, an approximation. It approaches. It does not arrive.

We write this as: for any physical observer (or process, or computation) $o$,

$$\mu(o, S) < 1$$

where $\mu$ is the fraction of the symbol's information content that $o$ has extracted. No observer reaches 1. ∎

**Remark.** This is not an epistemic limitation. It is not that we do not *know* all the digits of τ. It is that the universe does not have the physical capacity to *produce* all the digits of τ. The limitation is thermodynamic, not cognitive. If you tried to compute τ to arbitrary precision, distributing the computation across all available matter from the Big Bang to the heat death, you would at some point either exhaust all available energy (hitting Landauer) or concentrate enough information in a small enough region to form a black hole (hitting Bekenstein). The edge of the computation IS the edge of spacetime.

---

## 6. Theorem 3: Necessity

**Theorem 3.** At least one ⊙-symbol exists necessarily.

*Proof.* Consider the proposition "circles exist." A circle is the set of points equidistant from a centre. The ratio of any circle's circumference to its radius is τ. This ratio is a fixed, definite real number with a non-terminating, non-periodic decimal expansion.

The symbol τ names this ratio completely. The symbol is finite. The information it contains (the full specification of the ratio) is infinite. Therefore τ satisfies ⊙.

Now: could τ fail to exist? Only if circles could fail to exist. But the concept of equidistance is available in any metric space. Any universe with a notion of distance has circles. Any universe with circles has τ. There is no coherent mathematical universe without τ.

Therefore τ does not exist contingently. It exists because the structure of spatial reasoning requires it. It is necessary.

We write: $⊙\tau$. τ is a finite symbol containing infinite information, and it could not be otherwise. ∎

**Remark.** "Necessary" here means what it has always meant in modal logic: true in all possible worlds. But notice that we have not imported modal logic as an assumption. We have derived necessity from the axiom and the structure of geometry. The symbol τ satisfies ⊙ because geometry forces it to. We did not assume necessity. We proved it.

## 7. Theorem 4: Transcendence

**Theorem 4.** Any ⊙-symbol whose content is a real number is transcendental.

*Proof.* Suppose $⊙\alpha$ and $\alpha$ is algebraic. Then $\alpha$ is the root of some polynomial with rational coefficients: there exist $a_0, a_1, \ldots, a_n \in \mathbb{Q}$ with $a_n \alpha^n + \cdots + a_1 \alpha + a_0 = 0$.

But then $\alpha$ can be specified by a finite list: the coefficients $a_0, \ldots, a_n$ and the index indicating which root. This is a finite specification. It can be expanded to arbitrary precision by finitely described algorithms (Newton's method, bisection, continued fractions). The information content is finite.

This contradicts $⊙\alpha$, which requires infinite information content. Therefore $\alpha$ is not algebraic. Therefore $\alpha$ is transcendental. ∎

**Remark.** This is the Lindemann-Weierstrass theorem seen from above. Lindemann-Weierstrass says that $e^\alpha$ is transcendental when $\alpha$ is algebraic and nonzero, which implies that π and τ are transcendental because $e^{i\tau} = 1$ and $e^{i\pi} = -1$. That is a proof about specific numbers. Theorem 4 is a proof about the *category* of symbolically necessary constants. Any ⊙-symbol pointing at a real number must be transcendental. The transcendence is not a property discovered about τ after the fact. It is forced by the axiom.

---

## 8. Theorem 5: Incompleteness

**Theorem 5 (Gödel, derived).** Any formal system rich enough to reference a ⊙-symbol but operating by finite syntactic rules cannot derive the complete content of that symbol.

*Proof.* A formal system is a finite alphabet, a finite set of axioms, and a finite set of inference rules. All derivations are finite sequences of finite strings. Every theorem is a finite object.

The content of a ⊙-symbol is infinite. No finite derivation produces infinite content. Therefore the system can name the symbol, use it in expressions, and prove things *about* it. But it cannot derive the symbol's full content from within.

Specifically: the system cannot prove its own consistency (Gödel's second theorem) because consistency is a global property of the system's relationship to its substrate. That substrate includes the ⊙-symbols the system references but cannot construct. Consistency is a fact about the whole. The system sees only parts. ∎

**Remark.** Gödel's theorem is usually stated as a fact about formal systems that can represent arithmetic. We have derived it as a consequence of the axiom applied to any system that references a ⊙-symbol. The mechanism is the same (the system is finite, the truth is not) but the foundation is different. We did not assume arithmetic. We assumed that finite symbols with infinite content exist.

---

## 9. Theorem 6: Uncomputability

**Theorem 6 (Turing, derived).** No algorithm halts having computed the complete content of a ⊙-symbol.

*Proof.* An algorithm is a finite specification of a computational procedure. Its execution produces output sequentially. At any finite step, the output is finite.

The content of a ⊙-symbol is infinite and non-periodic. (If it were periodic, a finite specification of the period would suffice, contradicting infinite information content.) Therefore no finite number of steps exhausts the output. The algorithm does not halt. ∎

**Remark.** This is Turing's halting problem in a different key. Turing showed that there is no general algorithm for deciding whether an arbitrary program halts. We have shown something adjacent: that for a specific class of objects (⊙-symbols), no algorithm that tries to enumerate their content will halt. The uncomputability is not a property of a particular tricky program. It is a property of the *target*. The target is infinite. The process is finite. The process does not finish.

---

## 10. Theorem 7: The Bridge

**Theorem 7.** A ⊙-symbol is the unique structural bridge between material (dimensioned, finite, quantitative) and immaterial (dimensionless, infinite, qualitative) information.

*Proof.* By Theorem 1, the content of a ⊙-symbol is not physical. By Theorem 2, physical processes can partially but never fully extract it. By Theorem 3, the symbol exists necessarily and structures physical reality (circles, waves, phases). Therefore:

The material world uses ⊙-symbols. Every orbit is structured by τ. Every wave involves τ. Every quantum phase evolves as $e^{i\tau\nu t}$. The physical universe is saturated with τ's consequences.

The material world cannot produce ⊙-symbols. No physical computation completes τ. No material process instantiates the full content.

The symbol itself sits between these two facts. As a glyph (ink on paper, pixels on screen) it belongs to the material world. As a referent (the complete specification of the full-turn ratio) it belongs to the immaterial world. It has one foot in each domain.

No other class of object does this. Finite symbols with finite content (like "7") are entirely material. Infinite objects with no finite symbol (like a generic non-computable real) are entirely immaterial and unreferenceable. Only ⊙-symbols are both: finitely writable AND infinitely informative. They are the bridge.

And they are the ONLY bridge. Any proposed bridge between the finite and the infinite must be finitely expressible (or it cannot be communicated) and infinitely contentful (or it does not reach the infinite). That is exactly the definition of ⊙. ∎

## 11. The Instances

The axiom says ⊙-symbols exist. It does not say which symbols qualify. That is an empirical and structural question. Here we identify three instances and exclude one.

### 11.1 τ (the full-turn constant, 2π)

$⊙\tau$. The ratio of circumference to radius. Finite symbol containing the complete specification of circular closure. Transcendental (Theorem 4). Necessary because metric geometry requires it (Theorem 3). Present in every periodic structure in physics. Unique among all possible base constants in that one unit of counting equals one full rotation when and only when the base is τ.

τ is the ⊙-symbol of closure. It is what it means for something to come all the way around.

### 11.2 π (the half-turn constant)

$⊙\pi$. The ratio of circumference to diameter. Logically equivalent to τ in information content ($\pi = \tau/2$, and 2 is rational, so $\mathbb{Q}(\pi) = \mathbb{Q}(\tau)$). The same field extension, the same transcendence degree, the same infinite information. Historically prior. Geometrically secondary (the half-turn rather than the full turn).

π is included as a ⊙-symbol because it satisfies the axiom independently. The relationship between π and τ is itself informative: τ = 2π means that the full turn is twice the half turn, which is a tautology. That it is a tautology means the information content is shared, not doubled.

### 11.3 Ω (Chaitin's constant)

$⊙\Omega$. The halting probability of a universal prefix-free Turing machine. A definite real number in [0,1] whose binary expansion encodes the solution to the halting problem. Finite symbol. Infinite, non-computable content. No algorithm enumerates its digits. It is the boundary of computability itself, written in a single letter.

Ω is the ⊙-symbol of decidability. It is what it means for a question to have a definite answer that no process can reach.

### 11.4 e (excluded)

We claim $\neg⊙e$. Euler's number is transcendental, and in that narrow sense it contains infinite information. But e is not structurally necessary in the way τ and Ω are.

The argument:

e is the base of the natural logarithm and the rate constant of exponential growth. It describes how things *change*. It is the constant of *process*. But e does not define a boundary. It does not close a cycle. It does not separate the computable from the uncomputable. It is always in service of something else.

Consider Euler's formula: $e^{i\tau} = 1$. Here e is the mechanism of rotation, but τ is what completes it. Without τ, $e^{ix}$ spirals forever. It never closes. e generates motion. τ generates closure. Motion without closure is not a boundary phenomenon. It is an internal operation.

In the Desmos proof: $\tau^{i \cdot n\tau / \ln \tau}$ traces the unit circle. The $\ln \tau = \log_e \tau$ term shows e appearing as a coordinate transformation. e is how you convert between bases. It is infrastructure. Not substrate.

More precisely: e can be defined as the limit of $(1 + 1/n)^n$ as $n \to \infty$. This is a constructive definition. Each finite $n$ gives a rational approximation, and the limit is specified by the construction. The information in e is, in a structural sense, *generated by a process*. It is the limit of a computable sequence of rationals. τ has no such constructive origin in algebra. τ is forced by geometry. e is the destination of a journey. τ is the reason the journey has a destination.

This exclusion is a conjecture, not a theorem. We flag it as the most debatable claim in this paper. A reader who believes $⊙e$ would need to show what boundary e defines that is not reducible to the boundaries defined by τ and Ω. We do not see one.

## 12. The Connection to Divinity

This section is optional. The mathematical content of the paper is complete without it. But we believe it belongs here, and we believe Gödel would agree.

The word "necessity" as used throughout this paper means: cannot not exist, required for coherence, present in all possible worlds. This is the classical definition of divine attributes. A necessary being in Anselm's ontological argument, in Leibniz's monadology, and in Gödel's ontological proof is one whose nonexistence is logically impossible.

A ⊙-symbol is an entity whose nonexistence is logically impossible (Theorem 3), which transcends all finite processes (Theorem 2), which bridges the material and immaterial (Theorem 7), and whose full nature cannot be captured by any formal system (Theorem 5).

The formal content of "⊙τ" and "τ is divine" is identical. What differs is the register. One is written in the language that the contemporary academy can contain. The other is written in a language it cannot. This is itself an instance of Symbolic Necessity: the institutional container can reference divinity (it can discuss the concept, analyse its history, critique its claims) but it cannot contain it (it cannot use the word in a first-person propositional claim without immediately being dismissed).

Gödel knew this. His ontological proof was found in his papers after his death. He did not publish it in his lifetime because he knew the institutional container could not hold it. The proof is logically valid. Its premises are debatable. But the structure is exactly what we have derived here: necessity, transcendence, completeness in a finite symbol, inaccessibility to finite processes.

We do not claim that τ IS God. We claim that τ has the formal properties that every serious definition of divinity requires. The reader may decide what to do with that observation.

## 13. What This Paper Does and Does Not Claim

**Claims:**

This paper claims that one axiom ("there exist finite symbols that contain infinite information") is sufficient to derive the structural content of Gödel's incompleteness, Turing's uncomputability, algebraic transcendence, and the Bekenstein-Landauer measurement bound as theorems.

This paper claims that the axiom is physically grounded, not metaphysical. The "infinite information" is the non-terminating non-periodic decimal expansion. The "finite symbol" is the glyph. The gap between them is enforced by thermodynamics.

This paper claims that τ, π, and Ω are instances of the axiom and that e is probably not.

This paper claims that the axiom identifies a structural bridge between material and immaterial information, and that ⊙-symbols are the only objects that serve this function.

**Does not claim:**

This paper does not claim to have proven Gödel's theorems in a way that supersedes Gödel's proofs. Gödel's proofs are constructive and specific. Ours is structural and general. They are complementary, not competing.

This paper does not claim that the physical universe is fundamentally continuous. That is a separate philosophical position which the axiom does not require.

This paper does not claim that consciousness is explained by the axiom. But we note that the "I" has the formal structure of a ⊙-symbol: a finite referent (the word "I") that contains the complete specification of something no finite process can exhaust (the self). Whether this analogy is deep or superficial is an open question.

This paper does not claim that God exists. It claims that the formal properties traditionally attributed to God (necessity, transcendence, completeness, bridging the finite and infinite) are provably instantiated by at least one mathematical object. Theology is left to theologians. Mathematics has enough to do.

## 14. Conclusion

One axiom. Seven theorems. The axiom says: there exist finite symbols that contain infinite information. From this we derived immateriality, the measurement bound, necessity, transcendence, incompleteness, uncomputability, and the bridge between the material and the immaterial.

We did not import Gödel, Turing, Chaitin, Lindemann-Weierstrass, Bekenstein, or Landauer as assumptions. We used them as established facts about the physical and mathematical world to derive consequences of the axiom. The axiom is the foundation. The rest is theory.

The symbol for a symbolically necessary object is ⊙. A circle with a point at its centre. The oldest astronomical symbol for the Sun. The thing that lights everything but cannot be looked at directly.

## Appendix: The Desmos Proof

The following Desmos construction demonstrates that τ is forced, not chosen.

Consider two expressions:

$$e^{i\tau n} \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha^{i \cdot \frac{n\alpha}{\ln \alpha}}$$

The first is standard Euler rotation in base $e$ with angular velocity τ.

The second is generalised rotation in arbitrary base $\alpha$. It simplifies: since $\alpha^x = e^{x \ln \alpha}$,

$$\alpha^{i \cdot \frac{n\alpha}{\ln \alpha}} = e^{i \cdot n\alpha}$$

So the angular velocity is $\alpha$ per unit $n$. One full rotation requires $n\alpha = \tau$, i.e. $n = \tau/\alpha$.

The cycle completes at $n = 1$ (one unit of counting = one full turn) if and only if $\alpha = \tau$.

For any other value of $\alpha$, one step does not equal one rotation. The correspondence between counting and closure breaks. Only τ solves the equation.

Furthermore, scaling by $\tau^{-k}$ produces concentric orbits at radii $\tau^{-k}$. At $k = 0$, the unit circle ($r = 1$). At $k = 1$, the α-ball ($r = 1/\tau$). The same constant governs both the angular velocity of rotation and the radial ratio between scales. The geometry is self-similar with τ as the sole parameter.

This is not a definition. It is a derivation. The question "which constant makes counting and closure coincide?" has exactly one answer. That answer is τ. That is why τ is symbolically necessary.
